Lesson 1: The Gospel of God

April 1-7, 2025

Sunday: Introduction to Romans

The book of Romans, written by the apostle Paul around A.D. 57, is considered by many to be the most systematic presentation of Christian doctrine in all of Scripture. While Paul wrote this letter to address specific issues in the Roman church, God providentially designed it to unfold the great truths of salvation for all believers throughout the ages.

Paul wrote Romans from Corinth during his third missionary journey, just before returning to Jerusalem with the collection for the poor saints. At this point in his ministry, Paul had not yet visited Rome, though he expressed his desire to do so (Romans 1:10-13). The letter served as an introduction of Paul to the Roman believers and a clear presentation of the gospel he preached.

Key Verse for the Week:

"For I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes: first to the Jew, then to the Gentile." (Romans 1:16, NIV)

As we begin our study of Romans, we should approach it with both intellectual rigor and spiritual openness, recognizing that these truths are not merely theological concepts but life-transforming realities that shape our understanding of God, ourselves, and our salvation.

Monday: Paul's Greeting and Theme

In Romans 1:1-7, Paul introduces himself and summarizes the gospel message. He identifies himself as "a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle and set apart for the gospel of God" (Romans 1:1). This threefold description—servant, apostle, and set apart—establishes Paul's authority while emphasizing his submission to Christ.

The gospel Paul proclaims is not a human invention but "the gospel of God" (v. 1). It was "promised beforehand through his prophets in the Holy Scriptures" (v. 2), showing continuity between the Old Testament and the revelation of Jesus Christ. The gospel centers on God's Son, who was both fully human ("descended from David," v. 3) and fully divine ("declared to be the Son of God with power," v. 4).

Paul addresses his letter to "all in Rome who are loved by God and called to be his holy people" (v. 7). This inclusive greeting embraces both Jewish and Gentile believers, foreshadowing a major theme of the letter: the gospel is for everyone who believes.
